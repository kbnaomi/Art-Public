Book Review

Robert E. Lee

Allen C. Guelzo

Reviewed by Luke Brown
Genre Non-Fiction · Civil War
Date February 2026

How did this book come to be selected?

Robert E. Lee was given to me by my sister and AP reader in residence Hope after the completion of American Ulysses by Ronald White. A Christmas gift, I believe. It was on the to-read shelf for a few years to let the recency of Grant cool. It has been something of a goal to form a more educated opinion on the Civil War, and with the previous Non-Fiction goal of reading biographies of the three presidents that were also commanders of the U.S. Army completed, it was time to turn to the southern side of the War. It also comes on the heels of the Southern novel Absalom, Absalom.

◆

What is something you did not know about Lee that you do now?

To be fair, not much was known about him other than that he commanded the southern Army of Virginia. So almost everything. I knew he was a high ranking student with no demerits at West Point, but not that he was an engineer, and definitely not that he spent time in St. Louis rerouting the Mississippi to maintain the city's waterfront.

That he was related to George Washington by marriage. This is part of a larger topic of how Lee seemed to live his life under shadows — Washington, his own father Light Horse Harry, and maybe most of all his wife and the Arlington estate.

He spent his time after the war as President of a college, and reformed it into a very modern curriculum.

◆

How did the book inform your opinion of Lee?

The most pertinent insight into Lee's decision to resign from the U.S. Army, side with the South, and accept a position as an officer in the Confederate Army is of course his loyalty to Virginia. Which I was aware of — but loyalty to a state is a more abstract idea today. There was certainly more of a notion then that the states were in some ways independent, only in a somewhat loose binding with the larger union.

But even more than that is the simple reality of the logistics. What does it mean to be loyal to the state? More so than today, the state was his home, his family, his inheritance. He was driven to provide for his family, and leaving Virginia would have meant uprooting all roots of posterity, again so heavily tied to the Arlington estate. In a lot of ways Lee seemed to be in the wrong place and the wrong time - a thought that ran parallel to one I had reading Grant, in that neither man was really actively seeking out the position he wound up in.

Rating 4 / 5
